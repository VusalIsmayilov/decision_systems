## 1. Hero

**Thinking Mode:**  
Definition (Positioning)

**Concept:**  
Control over the data system as a distinct position vs fragmented state  

**Output:**  
Recognition of controlled vs fragmented → leads to Target Organizations  

**Allowed Signals:**  
- Control vs fragmentation  
- Single ownership vs distributed ownership  
- System vs disconnected parts  
- Stability vs variability  
- Control as a position  

**Forbidden Signals:**  
- Symptoms (no examples of inconsistency)  
- Causes or explanations  
- Organizational description  
- Industry references  
- Any triggers or conditions  

CONTENT:
Data can operate as a controlled system with clear ownership and coordinated execution—or remain fragmented across teams without a single point of control.
The distinction is not defined by tools or investment, but by whether the function is owned and run as one system or distributed across separate initiatives.
This defines whether the organization operates with control or fragmentation—and positions the data function accordingly.

## 2. Target Organizations

**Thinking Mode:**  
Situational (Triggers)

**Concept:**  
Organizations defined by complexity level and system presence  

**Output:**  
Recognition of organizational context → leads to Organizational Conditions  

**Allowed Signals:**  
- Mid-to-large organizations  
- Existing systems, vendors, teams  
- Dependency on data  
- Lack of unified ownership  
- “You are here if…” framing  

**Forbidden Signals:**  
- Symptoms (no decision inconsistency)  
- Structural explanation  
- Industry references  
- Fit / non-fit judgment  
- Any causal reasoning  

CONTENT:
You are operating at a level where data is already embedded across the organization.
Multiple systems, teams, and external vendors are in place. Data supports operations and decisions, but ownership of the data function is not unified.
The organization operates at scale—operational, commercial, or regulatory—where data is a core part of how the business runs.
No single role holds end-to-end responsibility for direction, structure, and execution as one system.
Budgets are allocated. Initiatives are active. Expectations are established.
But control of the data system is not unified.

## 3. Organizational Conditions

**Thinking Mode:**  
Structure (System Presence)

**Concept:**  
Structural conditions where fragmentation naturally emerges  

**Output:**  
Understanding of system formation → leads to Typical Symptoms  

**Allowed Signals:**  
- Distributed ownership  
- Multiple systems without control point  
- Governance not operational  
- Parallel initiatives  
- Vendor fragmentation  

**Forbidden Signals:**  
- Symptoms (no outcomes yet)  
- Industry references  
- Qualification logic  
- Solutions  
- Emotional or diagnostic framing  

CONTENT:
Data operates across multiple domains without a single control point.
Ownership is distributed across business units, IT, and vendors, each operating within defined scope rather than system-level control.
Systems have been developed over time for different purposes. They coexist but are not governed as a unified structure.
Governance frameworks exist formally, but do not consistently control how data is created, used, or changed.
Initiatives progress in parallel, without coordination at the system level.
Vendors contribute to delivery within fragmented ownership structures.

**Forbidden Signals:**  
- Causes (no “because”)  
- Structure (no system explanation)

- Solutions

CONTENT:
Decisions vary across teams without a consistent basis.
The same data leads to different conclusions.
Outputs are available, but not relied on with confidence.
Priorities shift without a stable reference point.
Execution progresses without consistent alignment to decisions.
Stakeholders spend time reconciling differences rather than acting.
Visibility does not translate into reliable decisions.

## 7. Non-Fit Criteria

**Thinking Mode:**  
Situational (Triggers)

**Concept:**  
Conditions where control is not applicable or not possible  

**Output:**  
Disqualification → leads to CTA or exit  

**Allowed Signals:**  
- Tool-only expectations  
- Early-stage / low complexity  
- No executive ownership  
- Advisory-only expectations  
- No need for system-level control  

**Forbidden Signals:**  
- Positive qualification  
- Industry references  
- Symptoms  
- Structural explanation  
- Soft language (must be explicit)  

CONTENT:
Not applicable where system-level control is not required or cannot be established.
Includes requests limited to tooling, delivery, or isolated technical outputs.
Early-stage or low-complexity environments without system-level dependency on data.
Situations without executive ownership or mandate for centralized control.
Advisory-only expectations without structural change.
Cases where the problem is defined as tooling, hiring, or vendor selection rather than system control.
